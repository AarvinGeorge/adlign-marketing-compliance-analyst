## The Fed’s latest decision: Rates on hold
On April 29, 2026, The Federal Reserve [kept interest rates unchanged](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/29/fed-meeting-today-live-updates-warsh-powell.html) for a third consecutive time. Federal Reserve officials kept the benchmark lending rate in a range of 3.5%–3.75%. If you’re wondering how this change affects your wallet, your savings, and your taxes, you’re in the right place. Here’s what these cooling interest rates mean for your money and what to expect when you file your taxes for tax year 2026.
## The Fed and interest rates: What happened and why it’s important
Beginning in March 2022, the U.S. Federal Reserve (Fed) raised interest rates [11 times](https://blog.turbotax.intuit.com/life/interest-rates-increased-to-fight-inflation-what-it-means-for-you-and-your-finances-52536/) in a row to combat high inflation. But the tide turned in September 2024, when the Fed began lowering rates as inflation cooled, cutting rates three times through the end of 2025. The most recent cut, announced in [December 2025](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20251210a.htm), reduced the target federal funds rate by a quarter of a point (0.25 percent), to 3.50%–3.75%.
This move matters because the Fed’s interest rate decisions affect many aspects of your financial life, from your mortgage payment to your credit card interest to how much you earn on savings accounts.
### What lower rates mean for you
  * **Borrowing gets cheaper.** Interest on mortgages, auto loans, and credit cards tied to prime rates may go down, enabling you to keep more money in your pocket each month.
  * **Student loans cost less.** Private student loans are tied to Fed rates too, so you may see lower interest rates and reduced monthly payments for these loans.
  * **Savings interest rates drop.** On the other side of the equation, the rate that banks pay you for funds held in high-yield savings accounts and CDs is also connected to the Fed rates, so these payouts may get smaller.


## **IRS inflation adjustments for tax year 2026**
### **Why it matters**
Even while interest rates hold, the IRS continues making inflation adjustments that affect your 2026 tax return. These changes are designed to help protect you from “[bracket creep](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/general/what-is-bracket-creep/c9esRHSsY),” which is when you move into a higher tax bracket than you were in the previous year even though your income didn’t grow as much as the rate of inflation.
Here are some of the key adjustments the IRS has made for tax year 2026:
### **Standard deduction increases**
The [standard deduction](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/tax-deductions-and-credits/what-are-standard-tax-deductions/L7oiVM1DH) increased for [tax year 2026](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-releases-tax-inflation-adjustments-for-tax-year-2026-including-amendments-from-the-one-big-beautiful-bill):
  * **Single filers:** $16,100 (up from $15,750 in tax year 2025)
  * **Married filing jointly:** $32,200 (up from $31,500)
  * **Head of household:** $24,150 (up from $23,625)


These adjustments automatically reduce your taxable income, potentially lowering your overall tax bill or increasing your refund.
### **Tax brackets shift upward**
Income thresholds for each [tax bracket](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/irs-tax-return/current-federal-tax-rate-schedules/L7Bjs1EAD) increase for tax year 2025, meaning you can earn more income before jumping to the next tax rate. For example, in 2025, the 24% bracket applies only to taxable income over [$105,700](https://www.irs.gov/filing/federal-income-tax-rates-and-brackets) for single filers. In 2025, the 25% threshold was just $103,350 for single filers. This change means more income is taxed at the lower 22% rate in 2026 than in previous years.
### **Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) expansion**
The [Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/tax-deductions-and-credits/earned-income-credit/L4lmLBNWa) provides significant relief for low-to-moderate-income earners. The maximum EITC for tax year 2026 increases to $8,231 for qualifying families with three or more children. This credit directly reduces your tax bill dollar-for-dollar and can result in a refund if it exceeds what you owe.
## **Other benefits**
### **Retirement contribution limits**
The retirement contribution limits [increased for tax year 2026](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/plan-participant-employee/retirement-topics-ira-contribution-limits).
  * [**401(k) plans**](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/401k-limit-increases-to-24500-for-2026-ira-limit-increases-to-7500)**:** $24,500 ($35,750 if you’re aged 60–63; $32,500 if you’re 50 or older)
  * **Traditional and Roth IRAs:** $7,500 ($8,600 if you’re 50 or older)


These tax-advantaged accounts serve as excellent opportunities to boost your retirement savings and reduce taxable income.
### **Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) and Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs)**
Annual contribution limits for HSAs and FSAs increased slightly for tax year 2026.
  * **[HSA contribution limits](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-25-19.pdf):** $4,400 for individual coverage, $8,750 for family coverage (an additional $1,000 if you’re 55 or older)
  * [**Healthcare FSA contribution limit**](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p15b.pdf#:~:text=Dependent%20care%20assistance%20exclusion%20from%20wages.%20For,filing%20separately\).%20See%20Exclusion%20from%20wages%2C%20later.)**:** $3,400
  * **Dependent Care FSA contribution limit:** $7,500 ($3,750 if married filing separately)


By contributing to these accounts, you set aside pre-tax dollars for healthcare and dependent care costs, reducing your taxable income while building a financial cushion for eligible expenses.
## **Smart moves to make right now**
### **Refinance strategically**
With interest rates dropping, refinancing your mortgage or student loans could save you thousands. Just make sure to run the numbers or seek guidance from a financial planning professional to make sure refinancing makes sense for your situation.
### **Maximize your deductions**
Track [deductible expenses](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/tax-deductions-and-credits/10-popular-tax-deductions/L9JesBWTQ) throughout the year to ensure you’re not paying more on your taxes than you have to. Expenses such as medical costs, [charitable donations](https://turbotax.intuit.com/personal-taxes/itsdeductible/), student loan interest payments, and mortgage interest could lower your taxable income if you itemize your deductions. Use apps and tools to track costs and donations throughout the year to make tax time easier. Staying organized can save you a lot of stress and money when filing your taxes.
### Boost retirement savings
Higher contribution limits for tax year 2026 mean you can shield more of your income from taxes. Along with reducing your current tax burden, even small increases to your 401(k) contributions can add up significantly over time.
### **Don’t leave money on the table**
Tax credits like the EITC, [Child Tax Credit](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/family/7-requirements-for-the-child-tax-credit/L3wpfbpwQ), and education credits enable you to save more of your money, so make sure you’re claiming everything you qualify for.
### **Take advantage of high-yield savings (while you can)**
Interest rates on savings accounts are still relatively strong, though they’re declining. If you have an emergency fund or short-term savings goals, high-yield accounts still beat traditional savings accounts considerably.
### **Review your withholding**
Major life changes, such as getting married, having a baby, or starting a new side hustle, can affect your tax withholding strategy. Too little withheld from your paycheck means a bigger tax bill in April; too much withheld means you’re giving the IRS an interest-free loan.
### **Watch for tax law changes**
Overall, the Federal Reserve’s decision to hold steady means the financial landscape that emerged from December’s rate cuts remains largely intact for now. Lower borrowing costs help your budget now and IRS inflation adjustments help protect you from bracket creep at tax filing time.
However, it’s important to stay proactive: Maximize your deductions and contributions, refinance if the math works, and keep careful records throughout the year. These small steps can add up to significant tax savings and better financial health overall.
